Who Is Responsible When an AI Kills?

Autonomous combat aircraft can already outmaneuver human pilots. The harder engineering and legal question is what happens next: when an AI weapon kills someone, who answers for it?

By Enrique Garcia  ·  ENGR 3100 Technical Writing  ·  Washington University in St. Louis

FIG 1 · X-62A VISTA flies over Edwards Air Force Base, California, April 30, 2024. U.S. Air Force photo by Richard Gonzales (DVIDS, Public Domain).

I. The Kill That No One Owned

Imagine a courtroom. A family has lost someone. The question before the judge is simple: who pulled the trigger? The soldier says it was the machine. The programmer says the algorithm did what it was trained to do. The commanding officer says he authorized a mission, not a specific strike. The manufacturer says the system performed within specifications. The courtroom falls silent. There is no one left to blame.

This is not a hypothetical from a legal philosophy seminar. It is the operational reality that defense attorneys, international law scholars, and human rights advocates say we are building toward with every autonomous weapons program currently in development. And it is no longer a distant concern.

In September 2023, the U.S. Air Force and the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency, better known as DARPA, conducted the world's first live dogfight between an AI controlled aircraft and a human pilot. The AI flew a modified F-16 known as the X-62A VISTA. The two jets closed to within 2,000 feet of each other at speeds exceeding 1,200 miles per hour. When officials were asked who won the encounter, they refused to say, pointing to national security concerns (Defense One, 2024; Breaking Defense, 2024). The details are classified. The capability is not. And the legal and ethical frameworks needed to govern that capability remain, as of this writing, entirely absent from binding international law.

We are deploying technologies that make lethal decisions while systematically removing the humans whose job judgment has always been.

This article examines three interlocking questions. First: what, technically, has been achieved in autonomous combat aviation, and how close are we to fully autonomous lethal systems? Second: what legal and ethical problems does autonomy create, and why are existing frameworks insufficient? Third: what geopolitical and structural barriers prevent the international community from governing these systems before they cause irreversible harm? The answers, taken together, describe a moving target. The technology is advancing on a much faster clock than the law, and the gap between the two is no longer merely academic.

II. From Remote Control to Machine Judgment

The history of unmanned military aviation is a history of gradually increasing autonomy. The MQ-9 Reaper, the most recognizable armed drone in the U.S. arsenal, is a remotely piloted aircraft. It is flown by a human operator, often from a ground control station thousands of miles away, who watches a video feed and makes the decision to fire. The aircraft does not decide. It executes. International humanitarian law has long been built around exactly this model, in which a human being with a weapon chooses a target and bears the legal and moral responsibility for that choice (Human Rights Watch, 2015). The Reaper, despite being unmanned, fits comfortably inside that older framework. A pilot in Nevada is still the one pulling the trigger.

The X-62A VISTA represents a categorical departure from that arrangement. The aircraft itself is a heavily modified F-16D Block 30 originally built in 1992, retrofitted with a custom flight control computer that lets engineers feed it any control law they choose. That flexibility is what made it the right testbed for DARPA's Air Combat Evolution program, which was launched in 2019. ACE was designed to do more than show that an AI could fly a fighter jet. The Reaper had already crossed that threshold years earlier in a remotely piloted form. The harder question was whether a machine learning agent could be trusted to fly one in close combat against another fighter, and whether human pilots in adjacent cockpits would come to accept its judgment.

The progression was deliberate. In August 2020, AI agents defeated experienced human pilots in simulated F-16 combat during the AlphaDogfight Trials. The result was treated, at the time, as a curiosity. Software beats software, and a simulator is not a sky. By December 2022, the program had migrated from simulation to the real aircraft, logging over seventeen hours of autonomous flight time at Edwards Air Force Base in California (DARPA, 2023). By September 2023, the AI was flying in live within visual range combat against a human piloted F-16 at closing speeds of roughly 1,200 miles per hour.

Two human safety pilots rode in the X-62A during those tests, and they had the authority to take over the aircraft at any moment. According to public accounts of the program, they never needed to do so (The Aviationist, 2024). The ACE team made over 100,000 changes to flight critical software across 21 test flights in less than a year, a rate of iteration that DARPA itself called "an unprecedented pace of development" (Flight Global, 2024). Secretary of the Air Force Frank Kendall, who flew in the X-62A himself in May 2024, called the 2023 trials "a transformational moment" in combat aviation (DARPA, 2024).

The presence of those safety pilots is a distinction that tends to get lost in the headlines. In the tests that have happened so far, humans retained the ability to intervene. The path from an AI that can be disengaged by a human in the cockpit to an AI that operates fully autonomously in combat is the threshold no international treaty currently defines. It is also the threshold the U.S. Air Force is openly preparing to cross. The Collaborative Combat Aircraft program, sometimes referred to as the loyal wingman concept, envisions fleets of cheaper autonomous fighters flying alongside crewed F-35s and the forthcoming Next Generation Air Dominance platform. Two prototypes from General Atomics and Anduril entered ground testing in 2024 and are scheduled to fly in 2026 (Defense One, 2024). These are not laboratory demonstrators. They are designed for production.

III. The Accountability Gap

The phrase "accountability gap" entered the academic literature through a 2013 article by Michael Schmitt and Jeffrey Thurnher titled "Out of the Loop: Autonomous Weapon Systems and the Law of Armed Conflict," published in the Harvard National Security Journal. Their argument was precise. When an autonomous system makes a targeting decision that violates the law of armed conflict, the chain of accountability that international humanitarian law assumes simply collapses. The programmer was not present at the strike. The commanding officer did not authorize the specific engagement. The soldier did not aim a weapon. The machine cannot be tried, imprisoned, or punished (Schmitt & Thurnher, 2013). What is left is a hole in the middle of the law, and the system designed to deter wartime atrocities loses one of the levers it relies on.

International humanitarian law, often abbreviated as IHL, is the body of rules that governs how parties to an armed conflict are permitted to fight. It is built on three interlocking principles. Distinction obligates combatants to tell military targets apart from civilians. Proportionality requires that any harm to civilians not outweigh the concrete military advantage gained. Precaution requires that reasonable measures be taken to keep civilian harm to a minimum. Each of these principles assumes, at its core, a human being exercising judgment in real time. The International Committee of the Red Cross, which is the formal guardian of these rules, has argued that IHL obligations are addressed to those who plan, decide upon, and carry out an attack, and that the lawful use of autonomous weapons therefore requires combatants to retain a level of human control over how those weapons function (ICRC, 2016, p. 10).

There are obstacles to holding individual operators criminally liable for the unpredictable actions of a machine they cannot understand, in particular because autonomous weapons systems that rely on AI may make determinations through opaque, 'black box' processes. — Human Rights Watch, 2025

Human Rights Watch elaborated on this concern at length in an April 2025 report. The document worked through how lethal autonomous weapons systems implicate at least six core obligations under international human rights law, including the right to life, the right to remedy, and the right to a fair trial. The most striking thread of the report concerned what Human Rights Watch calls the black box problem: even the engineers who design modern machine learning systems often cannot explain, after the fact, why a specific decision was made. The internal weights of a trained neural network are not a clear set of rules. They are millions of numbers tuned by exposure to data, and the relationship between those numbers and any particular output is not generally interpretable in human terms.

This opacity is not a passing engineering inconvenience that better tools will eventually fix. It is a structural feature of the machine learning approach that makes these systems so effective in the first place. A simpler, fully transparent decision rule could be audited line by line, but it would not be able to fly a fighter jet through a chaotic dogfight at 1,200 miles per hour. The same property that makes the system capable is the property that makes it legally illegible. That tension sits at the center of every current proposal for autonomous weapons governance.

IV. The Line May Already Be Crossed: Libya, 2019

In March 2021, a UN Panel of Experts released its final report on the armed conflict in Libya. The report described an incident in which a Turkish manufactured Kargu-2 loitering munition, a small quadcopter style drone capable of selecting its own targets, had been deployed against retreating fighters from the Libyan National Army. The panel wrote that the munition was programmed to attack targets without requiring data connectivity between the operator and the munition, and that the drone may have engaged its targets without a human operator authorizing the specific strike (UN Panel of Experts on Libya, 2021, p. 17).

It is worth dwelling on what the Kargu-2 actually is. The munition is produced by the Turkish defense company STM and weighs roughly 7 kilograms. It can be operated in a manual mode, in which a human operator selects targets through a video feed, or in an autonomous mode in which onboard computer vision software identifies, tracks, and engages targets that match certain visual signatures. The drone is also designed to swarm. STM marketing materials advertise that multiple Kargu-2s can be coordinated as a single attack package. The distinction between operating modes is a software setting that the operator selects before launch. The legal and moral difference between those two modes is, in effect, the entire project of international humanitarian law.

The UN panel was careful in its language, and many of the operational details of the Libyan engagement remain classified. The report did not state with certainty that an autonomous strike had taken place. What it described was strong circumstantial evidence that one might have. Even at that lower threshold, the implications were significant. If the report is accurate, this was the first publicly recorded instance of a lethal autonomous weapons system making an independent targeting decision in actual combat. No individual has been held responsible. No public investigation into autonomous decision making in that engagement has been conducted. The absence of accountability is itself informative. The international community lacks not only the rules but also the institutional capacity to investigate or adjudicate a strike that may have been made by a machine. Multiple academic and policy publications have since cited the Libya case as evidence that the accountability gap is not theoretical but operational (Perrin, 2025; Arms Control Association, 2025).

V. An Arms Race With No Referee

The geopolitical dynamics surrounding autonomous weapons bear uncomfortable similarities to the early nuclear arms race. The major powers each see strategic reasons to keep moving forward and structural reasons to distrust any framework that would slow them down. The United States, China, and Russia are all pouring resources into AI enabled military aviation. Each of the three sees the same set of advantages: faster reaction times, longer mission endurance, and the elimination of risk to human pilots in dangerous missions. Each of the three is also aware that adopting unilateral ethical constraints could leave it fielding less capable weapons than rivals who adopt no such constraints (Horowitz, 2018; Arms Control Association, 2025).

China's GJ-11 Sharp Sword is a flying wing stealth drone first revealed publicly during a military parade in October 2019. Russian sources have reported that a comparable system, the S-70 Okhotnik, has been fielded in operational testing alongside the Su-57 fighter, and Russian state media has shown footage of the aircraft conducting weapons release tests. Both systems are positioned as wingmen for crewed aircraft, performing roles that range from electronic warfare to strike. They are part of the same broader category as the U.S. Collaborative Combat Aircraft program, although the level of autonomy each system is granted in operation is not publicly known. What is clear is that each major air power is fielding its own version of the same idea, and that none of the three is waiting for a treaty before doing so.

The international forum most directly engaged with the governance of autonomous weapons is the Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons, often abbreviated as the CCW. The CCW's Group of Governmental Experts on Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems has been meeting in Geneva since 2014. In 2019, the group reached agreement on eleven guiding principles, which included full applicability of IHL and the maintenance of human accountability across the entire life cycle of an autonomous system (Arms Control Association, 2025). The principles were adopted by consensus. They are also non binding. The CCW operates by consensus rules, which means a single member state can effectively block any binding action. The United States has consistently resisted treaty language, taking the position that existing IHL already covers autonomous weapons and that a new instrument would be redundant (Lieber Institute, 2025).

In December 2024, the UN General Assembly passed Resolution 79/62 on lethal autonomous weapons systems. The vote was 166 to 3, with Belarus, North Korea, and Russia opposed and a small number of states abstaining. Symbolically, the result was a near unanimous statement that the international community considers autonomous weapons a serious problem worthy of regulation. Practically, a General Assembly resolution does not create binding legal obligations and contains no enforcement mechanism (Perrin, 2025). It is a vote of conscience. Some commentators have described the present situation as an Oppenheimer moment, a point at which scientists and engineers have built something whose implications they recognize, while political institutions have yet to catch up (Arms Control Association, 2025).

VI. The Humanitarian Case For and Against

It would be intellectually dishonest to treat autonomy in weapons systems as purely a threat. Proponents make a humanitarian argument that deserves to be taken seriously. Machines do not feel fear, fatigue, or rage. Soldiers under extreme stress have, throughout the recorded history of warfare, committed atrocities that no calm observer would have sanctioned. In theory, a well calibrated autonomous system operating inside clearly defined rules of engagement might make more consistent and discriminating decisions than a frightened nineteen year old in a chaotic urban environment. Secretary of the Air Force Frank Kendall made exactly this argument in reference to the ACE program, emphasizing that the AI used in the X-62A trials had complied with American norms for the safe and ethical use of autonomous technology (DARPA, 2024).

Capability is not permission. That a machine can make a targeting decision does not mean we have resolved who bears the moral and legal weight of that decision.

The counterargument operates on multiple levels. At the empirical level, autonomous weapons present unique risks to civilian safety by diminishing the ability of combatants to differentiate accurately between lawful and unlawful targets, particularly in the messy irregular warfare environments where most contemporary conflicts actually take place (Budustour, 2024). A system trained mostly in simulation may fail in unpredictable ways when it encounters a real scenario that falls outside its training data. A child carrying a stick may, to a computer vision system, look statistically similar to a fighter carrying a weapon. The cost of that error, in a system permitted to engage on its own judgment, is not academic.

At a deeper philosophical level, no amount of technical precision can resolve the underlying objection that the ICRC has now made central to its policy work. Using lethal force against a person without a human making the decision may violate human dignity in a way that transcends any specific IHL rule. The Martens Clause of international humanitarian law holds that civilians and combatants alike remain protected by the principles of humanity and the dictates of public conscience even in situations not explicitly covered by existing treaties (ICRC, 2016, p. 44). The question, on this view, is not whether an AI can fire accurately. The question is whether a killing without human moral agency can be legitimate at all. That is a question about what war is, not about how it is fought.

VIII. What Adequate Governance Would Look Like

The international community is not without options, and it is worth being concrete about what a serious governance framework would actually require. Drawing on the 2024 CCW rolling text, the American Society of International Law has identified several requirements that a future binding treaty could mandate. Systems must be predictable, reliable, traceable, and explainable. Meaningful human oversight must be maintained, especially for the act of selecting and engaging targets. Systems must be capable of being deactivated after deployment. Deployment in areas densely populated with civilians must be prohibited (Perrin, 2025). The list reads as a description of what the ACE program is currently striving to achieve internally as a matter of engineering and policy. The treaty version is the same idea, made universally binding.

The concept of meaningful human control is central to most governance proposals, but its definition remains contested. The core idea is that a human being must be able to understand, predict, and intervene in an autonomous weapon's targeting decisions in a way that is substantively meaningful and not merely nominal (Schmitt & Thurnher, 2013; ICRC, 2016). The engineering challenge is real. An AI combat aircraft can make tactical decisions in microseconds. Genuine real time human supervision in that environment is physically impossible. This suggests that meaningful human control, if it can be achieved at all, must be exercised upstream: in the design of engagement parameters, in the specification of prohibited target categories, and in the geographic and temporal constraints inside which the system is permitted to operate. The full moral weight of targeting decisions would then rest on choices made in the design phase, potentially months or years before any specific engagement takes place.

The ICRC has been explicit in its recent policy positions, calling for new international legally binding rules that would prohibit autonomous weapons that are unpredictable or designed to target persons, and place strict restrictions on all others (ICRC, 2026). What is required is, in other words, a treaty. Whether the political will exists to negotiate one is a different question entirely. The trajectory of the past decade suggests it does not, at least not yet. Whether public opinion, civil society pressure, or a sufficiently public failure of an autonomous system could change that political calculus is one of the open questions of this decade.

IX. The Empty Cockpit

The fighter jet banks hard. An algorithm assesses geometry, velocity, and threat probability in fractions of a millisecond. A targeting decision is made. No human directed it. No human could have stopped it in time. If someone dies, the courtroom will be as described in the opening of this article: full of humans, none of whom pulled the trigger.

The X-62A VISTA is an experimental aircraft operating under close human supervision. The Kargu-2 in Libya operated in an active armed conflict. The distance between those two situations is shorter than the international community has been willing to acknowledge. And the Collaborative Combat Aircraft program, the Chinese GJ-11 Sharp Sword, and the Russian S-70 Okhotnik together represent the next generation: systems designed not for testing but for deployment, not for demonstration but for war.

This article has argued four things. The accountability gap is real and structural, not a temporary problem of incomplete law. The Libya incident demonstrates that the gap has already produced at least one engagement in which it may have been exploited. The geopolitical dynamics driving autonomous weapons development actively resist the governance frameworks that could close it. And the technical capabilities being fielded are outpacing both legal and ethical infrastructure by a widening margin. None of these claims is comfortable, but each one is supported by the public record.

What is required is a binding international legal framework that specifies what meaningful human control means in practice, who bears accountability when an autonomous weapon causes unlawful harm, and what types of systems and deployment contexts are categorically prohibited. The engineering community has a particular role to play in this conversation. The systems being built encode ethical decisions at the level of design, and the engineers who build them are, in a meaningful sense, making targeting choices before any specific mission is ever authorized. That is not a comfortable thing to be told if you write code for a living. It is also true.

The cockpit is empty. The question is not whether that is technically impressive. It is whether the world is ready for what that emptiness means.
